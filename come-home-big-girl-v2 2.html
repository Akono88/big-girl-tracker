Diagnosis: Resource Guarding via Environmental Displacement
The Large Home ParadoxA larger home means more territory to "clear" for safety. She doesn't feel she owns the space yet, so she hyper-focuses on small, portable items (leash, toys, bowl).
The "Half-Time" Toddler TriggerThe 50/50 custody schedule prevents her from reaching homeostasis (steady state). Just as she relaxes, the "unpredictable element" (the child) returns — keeping her in hyper-vigilance, which physically manifests as Hemorrhagic Colitis (blood in stool).
Lethargy = Learned HelplessnessShe feels her space is being invaded and she cannot stop it, so she shuts down to conserve energy. This is a defense mechanism, not laziness.
The "Never Do" List
DO NOT punish the growlA growl is a warning. Suppress the growl → she skips the warning → goes straight to the bite.
DO NOT allow the 2.5-year-old near her while eating or in her crateHigh-risk bite zone. The toddler's unpredictable movements are perceived as a threat to her remaining "safe" resources.
DO NOT "free feed"Leaving food out all day increases anxiety — she feels she has to guard it 24/7.
DO NOT force "new faces" to pet herIf she is lethargic and tense, she is overstimulated. Forcing interaction leads to a fear-based bite.
Permanent Non-Negotiables
NEVER "reach and grab"If she has a toy or leash, never pull it from her. You must "trade up" — offer high-value treat (chicken) so she chooses to drop the item.
NEVER feed in a high-traffic areaHer bowl must be in a corner or crate where no one can walk behind her.
NEVER allow the toddler to "hug" herHuskies perceive hugs as restraint, which triggers fight-or-flight response.
NEVER skip the morning walkA walk is "job" time. Without a job, Huskies become "unemployed" — and unemployed Huskies become destructive or anxious.

🚨 Emergency Trade-Up

1
Toss a high-value treat (chicken, carrot) about 4 feet away from the guarded item.
2
Wait for her to move toward the treat. Do not reach yet.
3
